You finish your moisturizer, rinse the jar, and toss it in the recycling bin. That little triangle stamped on the bottom did its job — you feel good about it, and you move on with your day.

Here's the part nobody tells you: that triangle isn't a promise. It's barely even a label.

**What that symbol actually means**

The triangle with the number inside — the "chasing arrows" symbol — is a resin identification code. It was created in 1988 by the plastics industry, not by any recycling authority, to tell workers at sorting facilities what *type* of plastic an item is made from. Numbers 1 through 7 correspond to different resins: PET, HDPE, PVC, and so on.

That's it. That's all it was ever designed to communicate.

It says nothing about whether your specific city collects that plastic type, whether a nearby facility can actually process it, or whether it will end up anywhere other than a landfill. A jar can carry the symbol and still be functionally unrecyclable in your area.

Skincare packaging makes this worse. Pumps, droppers, and small jars often mix multiple plastic types, foil seals, and metal springs in one object — sorting machines can't easily separate them, so the whole thing gets rejected even if the main body is technically recyclable plastic.

**What's happening right now in 2026**

Regulators are starting to catch up. California's Truth in Labeling law, which began phasing in earlier this decade, restricts companies from using the chasing-arrows symbol unless the item is verified to be recycled at a meaningful rate in practice — not just in theory. Other states are moving toward similar standards. The EU has gone further, requiring standardized labeling that distinguishes genuinely recyclable packaging from what's merely "technically recyclable somewhere, sometime."

This is a real shift: for decades, the symbol let companies imply recyclability without ever proving it. That's starting to change.

**What you can actually do**

Look up your specific municipality's accepted plastics list — most cities publish one, and it takes two minutes to check. Rinse packaging before recycling; contamination is one of the biggest reasons otherwise-recyclable items get rejected at the sorting stage. And where you can, favor single-material packaging — a jar made entirely of glass or entirely of one plastic type is far more likely to actually get processed than a mixed-material pump.

The symbol isn't meaningless everywhere. It's just not a guarantee anywhere. Knowing the difference is the first step toward packaging choices that actually do what you think they're doing.

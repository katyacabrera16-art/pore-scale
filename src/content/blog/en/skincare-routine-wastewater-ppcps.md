All that water — carrying whatever was in your products — goes down the drain. We assume it gets cleaned up somewhere. Mostly, it does. But part of what you put on your skin is specifically designed to resist breaking down. That's the problem.

**The real story**

Wastewater treatment plants are remarkable pieces of infrastructure. They remove solids, kill pathogens, and return water to the environment in much better shape than it arrived in. What they weren't designed to capture is the chemical fingerprint of your morning routine.

A category of pollutants called PPCPs — pharmaceuticals and personal care products — includes the active ingredients in face washes, moisturizers, preservatives, synthetic fragrances, UV filters, and antimicrobials. Many of these compounds are biologically active by design: they were engineered to do something to a living organism. Some pass through treatment systems at removal efficiencies below 50%. A 2025 study on Canadian municipal wastewater found that numerous personal care product chemicals were still present in treated effluent, decades after research first flagged them as a concern.

Triclosan, an antibacterial that used to be in nearly every face wash, was eventually phased out of many products after studies showed it disrupts thyroid hormones in aquatic animals and may contribute to antibiotic resistance. But it took years of advocacy, research, and regulatory action to get there. Meanwhile, we're still using plenty of other compounds with similar persistence and not enough long-term data.

**2026 world context**

Environmental regulators in the EU and Canada are moving faster than the US on updating contaminant monitoring lists to include personal care chemicals. The EU's updated Water Framework Directive now identifies several cosmetic ingredients as priority pollutants. That means your moisturizer is becoming a water policy problem.

**What you can do**

You don't have to overhaul your entire routine. But a few swaps make a real difference. Shorter ingredient lists generally mean fewer unknowns going down your drain. Choosing fragrance-free formulas eliminates a broad category of compounds — synthetic musks, in particular, are persistent in water and bioaccumulate in fish tissue.

There's also a bigger-picture move: rinse-off products — cleansers, conditioners, exfoliants — have more direct environmental exposure than leave-on products. Scrutinizing those first is the highest-leverage place to start.

The water doesn't end with you. What you put on your skin doesn't stay there.
